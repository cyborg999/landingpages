SYSTEM ALERT! Is Your Gut Under Attack?

If you’re getting acid reflux on a daily basis, generally feeling “out of sorts”, and your breath is so stinky you’re running out of mints, then it’s time to point the finger. At your gut.

Your gut contains TRILLIONS of bacteria and that are both good and bad, but once you have a healthy balance of both you’re A-OK. The problem is that more and more people are experiencing issues with their gut on a daily basis.

A worldwide study by the American Gastroenterological Association showed that “more than 40% of persons worldwide have FGIDs (functional gastrointestinal disorders), which affect quality of life and health care use”.

This means that the symptoms of poor gut health are affecting millions of people. And these symptoms aren’t going away.

Do You Know What’s Growing In Your Gut??

Think of your gut as a garden that needs proper care and nourishment to thrive. If you don’t give it that care and attention, plants will wither and die, weeds will start to grow and take over, and it just won’t be functional anymore.

If you don’t look after your gut, a similar thing is going to happen and it will become vulnerable to harmful bacteria, h.pylori, viruses, and unwanted pathogens. Resulting in the symptoms commonly associated with an ill-functioning gut, such as:

  • Gas and bloating
  • Constipation and diarrhea
  • Bad breath
  • Acid reflux & GERD
  • Fatigue
  • Acne
  • IBS

2 Let’s Bust The Myth About Acid Reflux

There is a common misconception that acid reflux is primarily caused by excess stomach acid. So, people treat this by depleting their stomach acid even further with acid blocking medication, which A. only serves to band-aid the problem, and B. ultimately makes it worse. Still with me? Good. Because I’m about to explain EXACTLY what’s going on with your gut and how we can get rid of your acid reflux for good.

It all starts with this little valve called your Lower Esophageal Sphincter Muscle (LES). When you eat, this valve opens to let the food move into your stomach, then once the food has entered your stomach it starts producing acid which triggers your LES to close. All good so far, right? But, what happens when it doesn’t close? Acid reflux happens, that’s what.

When you don’t have enough stomach acid to signal that LES to close properly, the contents of your stomach reflux back up into your esophagus causing symptoms such as heartburn, a chronic cough, and difficulty swallowing.

Another primary cause of GERD is a H.pylori infection, which you can develop due to an overgrowth of small intestinal bacteria in your gut due to low stomach acid. HCI is crucial in the maintenance of a sterile stomach environment and it creates this by protecting against unwanted pathogens and providing a barrier against bacterial overgrowth. So when you have low stomach acid, you’re much more vulnerable to this problem.

As a result, studies have shown a link between H. pylori and GERD. When you have GERD, the food gets fermented in your gut instead of being digested and, amongst other symptoms, this does not make your gas smell pretty. If you’re suffering from acid reflux more than 3 times a week then it’s a good indication that this has progressed into GERD.

“Based on our findings, significant evidence suggests the potential role of HP infection in the development of GERD. Also, the current data provides sufficient evidence to define the relationship between GERD and HP infection.”
- Fatin R. Polat, MD and Sabriye Polat, JSLS
